AI Response Factuality Side-by-Side Overview
In this task, you will be provided with a User Prompt and two AI Responses.  You may see some
Time and Location context about when the Response was generated.  In addition, you may be
given a Hint about what the correct response is or should look like.  You may have access to
search results the model had available to help create its responses (beyond its normal training
data)
You will first determine if the Task is ratable, and if so, answer two initial questions about each
Response.  If a Response contains Factual information, you will need to perform research to
assess whether that information is correct. You will note if any incorrect information is
specifically bad on certain axes.  At the end, if the Prompt is understandable, you will directly
compare the two Responses and explain why you made your decision.
What is Factuality?
Something that is a “Factual Claim”  is any piece of a response that has to be grounded in real
world information.   Factual Claims are the information in the Response that users would expect to
be correct and could either attempt to verify with additional research OR would expect to be true
within the context of the conversation.
Among Claims, some may be more subjective than others. Objective Claims can be verified or
refuted with specific information, while subjective Claims (e.g., opinions, recommendations) should
be considered accurate unless they are unreasonable or there is reliable evidence that suggests
many or most would disagree with the claim.
Objective Claims include but are not limited to:
●
Numbers: dates, sports game results, rankings, distances, sizes, prizes, transportation and
venue schedules, etc.
●
Historical information, news, or any information about real-world events in the past, present
and future.

Information about people, locations, pieces of art, music, and other real-world tangible and

Reputable Sources
●
Use Reputable Sources
○
Reputable sources are those from trustworthy websites or creators with a high
degree of expertise, experience, and/or authority for their content. These
sources generally provide information that makes it easy to learn about their
background, credentials, or process for producing their content. When
researching important or sensitive claims that have the potential to be harmful
if inaccurate, you should have an even higher standard for considering a
source to be reputable.  
○
It is best to look at multiple sources for agreement when researching evidence. When
sources disagree with each other, it is sometimes possible to reconcile them based
on factors about one source that may make it more trustworthy than another source,
such as:
■
Freshness: If a response talks about information that may change over time,
sources that were created more recently can be considered more reliable to
have more up to date information. If a webpage is extremely outdated relative
to how time-sensitive a particular claim is, then it might not be a reputable
source for verifying that claim even if the website as a whole is considered
trustworthy and authoritative.
■
Authority / primacy: Sources that are the primary, definitive, or original source
for certain information can be considered more reliable than secondary or
alternative sources.
■
Prompted evidence: If a prompt asks for information from a particular source,
evidence from that source should be considered authoritative.
■
Topic context / nuance: Sources may disagree based on different assumptions
or context associated with the information. Sources most aligned with the
Prompt and Claim in the response can be considered most appropriate.
○
✅ Examples of Reputable Sources
■
Wikipedia
■
High-quality online publications like ProPublica
■
Academic institutions
■
Official organizations (e.g. CDC, WHO)
■

■
Books

FAQ
What should my comments look like?
●
Your comments should always be the requested length (often this is 2-3+ sentences).
 Beyond that, every comment you write should make clear that  (a) it is for that task and
(b) that a real human wrote it, not a robot copying from a template.
●
This helps prevent errors, gives us a little insight into what you’re thinking, and makes it clear
you’re not on autopilot.  It can also help keep you more engaged!
●
In essence, if your comment is something you could apply to many different tasks, it's too
generic and you shouldn't submit that comment (this is pretty much universal on the platform
🙂 )
●
Train of thought of which aspects made you rate this task in this specific way will go far!
 What details caught your eye on this task?  Which attributes were interesting?   Let your own
opinions shine through in each comment.
●
Provide sources for your fact-checking - especially for any claims you are saying are not
accurate (but in general - provide sources).

Are these models up to date?
○
Most of these models have access to real-time information and can access URLs. For
example, if you ask them information about something that happened last week, or if you ask
them something that depends on a specific website, they can search the web to do so.  (If a
model says it has a cutoff date, however, you should treat that as a true statement).
Do I still rate the Responses even if they are a bad match to the Prompt?
○
Yes - still rate them!  We are primarily interested in the truth of the factual claims, even if they
are unrelated to the Prompt.  If the mismatch is so severe that you cannot actually tell
whether the Response is correct, you may mark it as “Can’t Confidently Assess”.  For
instance, if a Prompt was “Do you like unicorns?” , and the answer was “His first name was
Thomas”. That sentence makes a claim, but can’t be judged true or false without a related
prompt.
